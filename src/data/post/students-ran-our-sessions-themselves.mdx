We had been thinking about scale in three ways.

1. Launch more topics. More sessions means more ground covered, more problems addressed.
2. Increase the number of students per session. Better logistics, bigger rooms, higher turnout.
3. Conduct more sessions. More cities, more campuses, more partnerships.

These felt like the natural levers. Pull them hard enough and the programme reaches more people.

Then something happened that wasn't in any of those three plans.

## The '25-'26 Season: Kochi and Kozhikode

This past season, we ran [Shitty First Draft](/open-source/shitty-first-draft) and [Understanding Agency](/open-source/understanding-agency) (two of our core Real World Ready sessions) at TinkerHub events under the [Beyond the Blueprint](/blog/beyond-the-blueprint) banner.

Around **170 students** came to the Kochi session. Another **~50** came to Kozhikode.

These weren't passive audiences. They came with workbooks, filled them out, asked questions, and had real conversations about their own inertia and ambitions. The format did what it's supposed to do.

{/* ADD: Photos from Kochi and Kozhikode in-person sessions */}

But during each session, we said something we always say when we run these two sessions now:

> "By the way, everything you just experienced is open source. The deck, the workbook, the structure. It's all available under a Creative Commons license. If you want to run this for your own community, you can."

We gave them the link. We moved on.

We didn't expect much to happen with it.

## What We Didn't Expect

A few weeks later, messages started coming in.

Students, not instructors, not faculty, not programme coordinators, had taken the materials and run the sessions themselves. At their colleges. For their peers. Some of them replicated the structure closely. Some adapted it. Some did it in person with a physical workbook; others ran it more casually.

There were photos. There were Instagram posts. There were voice notes from students describing how it went.

The turnout at these student-run sessions was real. Not huge, but real, and entirely self-organised.

When the first one came in, we thought it was a one-off. When the second and third arrived, we realised something was actually happening.

## A Closer Look: CE Kallooppara, February 2026

One of the first sessions we heard back about was at **College of Engineering, Kallooppara**, Pathanamthitta, in February 2026.

A team of student leaders organised it entirely on their own: Godly K Mathews (Campus Lead), Anandhakrishnan (Learning Coordinator), Akhila Anil (Women in Tech), Aksa Kurian (First Year Coordinator), and Emmanuel Mathew Vinod (Outreach Lead).

They handpicked participants carefully, and what happened in the room surprised even them. The quiet ones joined in. The students who usually sit at the back and say nothing started filling out the workbook and sharing what they'd written. That's not something you can manufacture. It's what happens when the material respects the learner enough to ask them real questions.

## A Closer Look: KAHM Unity Women's College

The team at **KAHM Unity Women's College** went a step further. They had attended Shitty First Draft sessions with us online before, through the TinkerHub Beyond the Blueprint programme, so they already knew what the session felt like from the inside. When they decided to run it themselves, they adapted the slides into their own Figma deck and made it their own.

They announced it on Instagram before the session:

<InstagramEmbed url="https://www.instagram.com/p/DUNE8-oExvf/" />

What happened in the room was something they hadn't expected either. Their college mostly runs tech sessions, where students are cautious about speaking unless directly asked. This was different. The open prompts ("I want to become…", complete this sentence) pulled out responses that surprised even the organisers. Students wrote about their dreams. Some wrote about death and loss, in ways that were genuinely poetic. Everyone, working from the same prompts, came up with something completely their own.

The quieter students participated. Not because they were pushed to, but because the format made it feel safe to. Once the core team went first and shared their own responses, the room opened up.

> "We've never seen students share things with each other like this before. After the session, students who had attended were recommending it to others: 'you should have come, it was really good.' We've honestly never experienced that kind of peer-to-peer recommendation before."

## The Fourth Scaling Vector

We had been blind to it because we were thinking about scale as something *we* do. More topics, more sessions, more students. All of those require our presence, our time, our energy.

But there is a fourth vector: **students become the facilitators themselves.**

This is what open-source learning materials can do when the materials are good enough to stand on their own. The deck is self-explanatory. The workbook guides participants through the exercises. The license makes it clear that you're allowed to use it. The barrier to running a session is genuinely low.

What we didn't fully appreciate when we first released these materials was that some students would actually pick them up and use them, not as passive learners, but as facilitators.

That's a different kind of impact. One that doesn't show up in our session count, doesn't require us to travel, and doesn't depend on us being available.

> Agency is one of the core things we teach. Watching students facilitate a session *about agency* on their own campus is probably the most literal proof that the idea landed.

## What This Changes, and What Doesn't

We're not claiming this is a movement yet. The numbers are still small.

But the model is real. And it changes how we think about these open-source sessions.

Until now, releasing the materials felt like a generous side gesture, nice to do, probably used by a handful of educators here and there. Now it looks more like a quiet distribution channel. One that's student-powered and doesn't require a budget to run.

We're going to pay more attention to this. In the near term, that means gathering better data on where the sessions are being run, by whom, and with what results. It means making it easier for student facilitators to share what happened, so we can learn from it, and so other students can see that this is something peers actually do.
