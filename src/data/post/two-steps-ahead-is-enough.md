Across enough Real World Ready sessions, a pattern shows up often enough that it stops looking like a coincidence: students who've actually solved something stay quiet about it, and the students who most need to hear it never do, because both sides are running the same wrong calculation.

## The permission problem

A student solves something, small or large, and wants to talk about it. Before they do, a quiet calculation runs: am I successful enough to say this out loud, do I have the standing to give advice. On the other side of the same conversation, a listener runs a matching calculation: is this person impressive enough for me to actually take their advice seriously.

Both calculations are wrong, and both are common enough that they quietly shape what gets said and what gets heard in a room full of students. The result is a kind of silence that looks like humility but functions like gatekeeping. People wait for a title before they'll share what worked. People wait for a credential before they'll believe what they're told.

The best advice we've watched students give each other did not come from someone with ten years of experience. It came from someone two steps ahead: a person one semester further along, who had solved the exact problem the other person was stuck on, last month, not last decade.

## What it takes to fix it isn't a mandate

You can't fix this with a policy. Nobody responds to being told "share more openly" from the top. What actually moves the needle is smaller and more specific than that: a handful of people willing to be vocal advocates, willing to say "this worked for me" without a résumé to back it up, and willing to keep saying it even when someone rolls their eyes.

That's an act of bravery, not expertise. And it's contagious in a very literal way. When enough people say the honest, unimpressive version of what worked for them, the people who default to mockery lose their audience, mostly because there's nothing left for them to gain by doing it.

We've seen this work at a much smaller scale than "culture change" would suggest. One of the first things we push in a session is as basic as showing up on time. Punctuality isn't the point on its own; a handful of concrete, consistently modeled behaviors do more to shift a room's norms than any amount of talking about norms in the abstract. Three things, done reliably, spread further than a thousand things said once.

The students we're trying to build this for don't need a celebrity to learn from. They need someone close enough to reach, a semester or two ahead, willing to talk about what actually worked and what didn't. That's a much lower bar than most students think it is, and it's the entire reason the bar needs to stay low.
