In 2024, we created [Beyond the Blueprint](/blog/beyond-the-blueprint)—our workshop series for college students to develop an entrepreneurial mindset—delivered in collaboration with Tinkerhub, Kerala.

At the time, we were trying to test something that felt almost counterintuitive.
Could online sessions still create meaningful learning experiences for college students who had already spent years exhausted by Zoom calls and Google Meet classrooms during the pandemic? Students who had missed out on one of the most important parts of education itself: learning socially, alongside peers.

More specifically, we were testing two ideas.
1. Can students meaningfully learn non-technical but deeply important life and career skills—ideas like agency, systems thinking, creativity, and getting started —through short, self-contained, activity-driven sessions lasting just 60–90 minutes?
2. Can these ideas be effectively taught entirely through remote online sessions?

## The Experiment Worked
In the first year, we proved—without a doubt—that both ideas could work.
Across 36 campuses, we conducted weekly sessions with groups of 15–25 students on topics as diverse as:
agency [Understanding Agency](/open-source/understanding-agency), 
applying systems thinking to career design,
and how to get started on anything [Shitty First Draft](/open-source/shitty-first-draft).
The response from students was immediate and unmistakable.
Students often told us that the sessions opened up entirely new ways of thinking about themselves and their futures. Many found tools and frameworks they could immediately apply in their own lives. And perhaps most unexpectedly, attendees repeatedly told us they had never imagined an online session could feel this engaging, interactive, or alive.
That proof of concept gave us confidence—not only in the ideas themselves, but in the format and methods we had developed.
And that led us to the next challenge:

## How Do You Scale This?
In the 2025–26 academic year, we decided to expand the collaboration significantly.
300 students across 58 campuses
At first glance, scaling seemed straightforward. We considered three obvious approaches:
- increase the number of students per session,
- increase the number of sessions, or
- increase the number of topics.

But a simple thought experiment quickly revealed the limitations.
The live, interactive nature of the sessions was precisely what made them powerful. Students were not passive listeners—they were active participants, contributors, and co-creators of the experience.
That created natural constraints.
There are only so many live sessions that can be conducted in a week without sacrificing quality. And there is only so far you can increase the size of a session before meaningful interaction disappears and the experience collapses back into a one-sided seminar.
That would have fundamentally violated the original ethos of Beyond The Blueprint.
This posed a genuine problem for us.
Were we fundamentally limited in the number of students we could reach?
 Could we never realistically reach the 10,000 students we had naively dreamed about?
And even 10,000 students, in a country with over 4 crore college students, is still only a drop in the ocean.

## The Hint Came From the Students

The answer emerged organically from the students themselves.
As participants became excited by the ideas and experiences in the sessions, many naturally began discussing them with friends. We started hearing stories of students teaching concepts from the workshops to their peers and applying the techniques in their own day-to-day lives.
That gave us an idea.
What if we formalized this process?
What if students themselves became the carriers of these ideas? 

## Open-Sourcing the Sessions

We first decided to open-source our materials—making our workbooks and slide decks freely available to anyone interested in conducting sessions.
But then we pushed the idea one step further.
What if we trained students to facilitate these workshops themselves on their own campuses?
That was the beginning of our Train the Trainer (TTT) model. 

## Building the Train the Trainer Model

There were obvious challenges.
The first challenge was content.
We did not want students to face the intimidating task of starting from a blank slate. So we designed our workbooks and slide decks to function as ready-to-use facilitation tools—materials that could be adapted, remixed, and used as needed.
But there was a second, more subtle challenge.
Many of our sessions rely heavily on stories and examples from our own lives. Those stories work because they are lived experiences. They cannot simply be copied and retold by someone else.
So we redesigned the sessions themselves.
Instead of creating rigid scripts, we intentionally created open spaces within the workshops where facilitators could bring in their own experiences, examples, and perspectives. This allowed each facilitator to reinterpret the session through their own worldview and lived reality.
The goal was not replication.
The goal was adaptation.
We wanted to provide enough scaffolding that students could confidently take these ideas back to their peers—without having to build everything from scratch.
In essence, we were trying to create a self-sufficient starter kit: a plug-and-play framework for anyone who wanted to start meaningful conversations around these ideas. 

## Rubber Hits The Road

In the 2025-26 academic year, we moved forward our in-person sessions to the winter camp. This gave us earlier opportunities to work directly with potential facilitators and gradually ease them into their new roles.
At the winter camp, we piloted our first formal TTT module using our Shitty First Drafts workshop.
Participants experienced the session in the same activity-based format we typically use, complete with workbooks and collaborative exercises. But alongside that, we also unpacked how the session worked structurally:
- how to facilitate conversations,
- how to personalize examples,
- how to create narratives, and
- how to invite students and market the sessions on campus.

We trained 58 student learning coordinators—35 in Kochi and 23 in Kozhikode.

The response was overwhelmingly positive.
Many students said they felt excited and newly confident about conducting sessions in their own campuses. Soon after, several student-led Shitty First Drafts sessions began emerging independently across campuses.
Meanwhile, our open-source materials have already been downloaded and used more than 50 times. 

## The Way Forward

The Train the Trainer model has now moved from idea to proof of concept.
We have shown that these experiences can scale—not by centralizing delivery, but by decentralizing ownership.
And importantly, the sessions are not becoming carbon copies of the originals.
Every facilitator brings their own personality, context, worldview, and lived experience into the room. What emerges is not replication, but reinterpretation. Each session becomes a creative adaptation rather than a duplicate.
That, we believe, is the only meaningful way to scale something deeply human.
Not through uniformity, but through variation.
 Not through control, but through ownership.
And that is how we hope to eventually reach the thousands of students we still have not met yet.

## A Closer Look: CE Kallooppara, February 2026

One of the first sessions we heard back about was at **College of Engineering, Kallooppara**, Pathanamthitta, in February 2026.

A team of student leaders organised it entirely on their own: Godly K Mathews (Campus Lead), Anandhakrishnan (Learning Coordinator), Akhila Anil (Women in Tech), Aksa Kurian (First Year Coordinator), and Emmanuel Mathew Vinod (Outreach Lead).

They handpicked participants carefully, and what happened in the room surprised even them. The quiet ones joined in. The students who usually sit at the back and say nothing started filling out the workbook and sharing what they'd written. That's not something you can manufacture. It's what happens when the material respects the learner enough to ask them real questions.

## A Closer Look: KAHM Unity Women's College

The team at **KAHM Unity Women's College** went a step further. They had attended Shitty First Draft sessions with us online before, through the TinkerHub Beyond the Blueprint programme, so they already knew what the session felt like from the inside. When they decided to run it themselves, they adapted the slides into their own Figma deck and made it their own.

They announced it on Instagram before the session:

<InstagramEmbed url="https://www.instagram.com/p/DUNE8-oExvf/" />

What happened in the room was something they hadn't expected either. Their college mostly runs tech sessions, where students are cautious about speaking unless directly asked. This was different. The open prompts ("I want to become…", complete this sentence) pulled out responses that surprised even the organisers. Students wrote about their dreams. Some wrote about death and loss, in ways that were genuinely poetic. Everyone, working from the same prompts, came up with something completely their own.

The quieter students participated. Not because they were pushed to, but because the format made it feel safe to. Once the core team went first and shared their own responses, the room opened up.

> "We've never seen students share things with each other like this before. After the session, students who had attended were recommending it to others: 'you should have come, it was really good.' We've honestly never experienced that kind of peer-to-peer recommendation before."

## Bring a Free Session to Your Community

If you're running a student community, a campus club, a fellowship, or any kind of programme for young people, we will come and run a free session for you.

Shitty First Draft and Understanding Agency are the two sessions we've open-sourced, and they're also the two we're most willing to run in person for communities we haven't worked with yet.

Why free? Because we're trying to build relationships, not sell one-offs. We want to understand your community's context, see how students respond, and figure out together whether there's a deeper collaboration worth building.
