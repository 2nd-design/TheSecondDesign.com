## What she was doing while everyone else was studying

In college, most of us are figuring out how to pass exams, what electives to pick, and how to make the 8:00 a.m. class. Aksa Rose was doing all of that too. But in the middle of it, she also fine-tuned a language model, built a viral AI product, ran hack nights out of a hostel when she couldn't get a venue, asked a stranger on the internet for GPU credits — and actually got them — and built a women-in-tech community at her college. Not because somebody asked her to. Because she looked around, saw a gap, and filled it.

This is not a story about talent. It's about specific decisions made at specific junctures — many of them made before she felt ready.

## First decision: don't just be a student

Aksa started college expecting to find people building things. She didn't. Most people around her were focused on the academic side. She wanted more than that.

That's when she found TinkerHub. She got exposed to the foundation-level people — saw how passionate they were — and decided she wanted to be part of it. She volunteered. She got in. Her early days weren't about code at all: they were about building community, organising events, and getting like-minded people into the same room. That was enough to start.

She eventually became Project Coordinator of Saturday HackNight at TinkerHub Foundation — not because she was asked, but because she kept showing up and caring more than most.

## Second decision: go, even when they say no

The high-agency moment she names first isn't a project. It's a trip.

In her second year, she heard about an India Partner event she wanted to attend. Her parents said no. She convinced her uncle. She figured out the logistics herself and bought a separate ticket. She went.

At that event, she met Abhiram — a senior who gave her one piece of advice she still remembers: format your Git commit messages properly. Not because it matters to you, but because it's the first thing a professional developer notices. It signals that you think like a developer, not a student.

One conversation. One trip she almost didn't take. Still with her.

## The loop she runs

After the community phase, Aksa moved into coding — frontend first, then an internship in frontend, then the realisation that she hated it. She dropped it and moved to AI/ML.

The learning process she describes isn't a curriculum. It's a loop:

**Learn something. Build something with it. Post it publicly. Have conversations. Learn more.**

She posts on LinkedIn not because she's sure she's right, but because posting surfaces the gaps. Someone messages her with a different perspective. They debate it. She updates her understanding. Even a post with fewer than ten reactions was worth it — at minimum, it forced her to articulate what she thought she knew.

She had self-doubt through third and fourth year. She was going deep into deep learning without knowing if the industry would value it, without knowing if she could do it, without a clear signal that any of this was working. She kept going anyway — not out of discipline, but out of curiosity. She gets genuinely happy when she learns something new. That turned out to be enough.

## Man Slater

Man Slater is an AI product that translates what women say into what they actually mean. It's a fun project. That's the point.

She built it, shipped it before it was perfect, and posted about it. It reached 70,000 people. Over 1,000 reactions. 3,000+ users. People started asking if she wanted to intern with them. Her second internship came directly from that post.

She didn't know it was going to go anywhere. She was just building something she found interesting and sharing it the way she always did. The difference was that two to three months before Man Slater, she had been posting consistently — every week, something new — because that's when her learning had accelerated enough that she always had something worth sharing.

She also built Extreme Title RAG, a tool for generating better document titles using retrieval-augmented generation — 500+ users. Every project goes through the same loop: build, release before it's ready, talk to the people who use it, change direction if something more interesting appears.

"I'm more of a learner than a builder," she says. "I build for fun."

## The women in tech angle

Aksa is going to be the only woman on her engineering team. She knows this. She feels proud and anxious at the same time.

Proud, because she's breaking a stereotype she finds genuinely irritating — the one where women in tech teams are assumed to be doing design or frontend. Anxious, because she wants a fellow woman in the room with her.

She built the WIT (Women in Tech) community at her college for the same reason she did everything else: she saw something missing and built it. Not as a service project. Because she wanted it to exist.

## What the decisions have in common

She went to an event her parents told her not to attend. She posted on LinkedIn before anyone was watching. She released projects before they were finished. She moved from frontend to deep learning mid-internship. She asked strangers for GPU credits.

None of these required permission. All of them required deciding to go anyway.

That's the whole thing.
