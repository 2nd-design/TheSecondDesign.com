## The subject she said she'd never study

In 12th grade, Abina told anyone who'd listen that she would never do a B.Tech. Her parents disagreed. She ended up at Jyothi Engineering College doing exactly that, an artificial intelligence and data science degree she hadn't chosen.

First year confirmed the fear. She'd been a strong student in school. By the end of her first year she had a backlog, a failed subject, and a feeling she describes simply as heartbreak, the first time she'd been confronted with the gap between who she thought she was and what was actually happening.

## Trying every community like it's a Sadhya

She didn't fix this by studying harder. She fixed it by looking sideways.

A senior a few years ahead of her was interning in her seventh semester while running multiple student communities at once. Nobody else in the college was doing that. Abina decided she wanted to be her, and turned the decision into a habit: she started showing up to every community her college had, IEDC, FOSS, NeoLearn, TinkerHub, one after another, the way you try dish after dish at a Sadhya to find out what you actually like. She admits it looked chaotic from the outside. It was a search, run in public, without waiting to know the answer first.

A senior at IEDC noticed her asking questions in a lab one day, pulled her aside, and offered her a post on the spot. That was the first door. The one that mattered more opened a few months later, at a NASA Space Apps Challenge, where her four-person team built a website to showcase open-science projects and won the Innovation Excellence Award. It was also the first time in her life she wrote a line of code, in a hackathon, because the problem needed solving and nobody was going to solve it for her.

## What TinkerHub gave her that a classroom didn't

By the middle of second year, TinkerHub was the community that stuck. Her description of why is specific: it is a place where you're allowed to ask a stupid question and not be made to feel stupid for asking it. She'd walk in, sit down confused, and a stranger volunteering there would notice and just start explaining. No one made her feel behind for not already knowing.

She applied to be a campus lead. The interview process paired her with her closest friend, and a TinkerHub organiser asked each of them, in front of the other, what the other person did better. Her friend answered first, and gave the position away, saying Abina was the one who belonged there. Abina got the role. She still describes that moment as the most complicated feeling she's had: proud and guilty at once.

## The event her principal said no to, three times

As campus lead, she wanted to bring TinkerHub properly to her own college with an orientation event she called Chai and Pups. Her plan was simple: explain what TinkerHub actually is, and let first-years opt in before they got locked into three more years of not knowing it existed.

She was confident enough that she went to a bakery and ordered 200 cups of chai and 200 puffs before she'd been given permission to run the event at all.

Her principal rejected the proposal. Then rejected it again. She recruited her department's teachers as allies, went class to class pitching the idea in person, and came back with data: 70% of the students she'd spoken to wanted to join. Her principal still said no. She needed money for the snacks she'd already ordered, so she went to the Computer Society of India's campus chapter, who had an unspent events budget and no events to spend it on, and got them to fund it. It took a full faculty meeting, with teachers arguing her case, before the principal finally relented.

## When the room emptied out anyway

The weekly sessions that followed didn't stay full. She prepared obsessively for the first one, writing and rewriting talking points on sheet after sheet of paper until a teacher told her to stop and just go in. By week six, attendance had thinned. Students drifted back to their regular Friday lunch instead.

So she changed the format. TinkerHub runs a hackathon called Useless Projects, built around the idea that you make something deliberately pointless and it's still worth making. Her principal wouldn't approve an in-person version, so she moved it online, over Google Meet, expecting a handful of teams. Twelve showed up, several of them unregistered students who'd heard about it and asked to join anyway. She added a presentation round so participants would practice pitching, not just building, and brought in an outside judge from another college to keep it honest. Two teams from her college won prizes they wouldn't have had a shot at if she'd cancelled the event when attendance dropped.

What actually changed her principal's mind wasn't the data or the faculty meeting. It was the fact that it worked.

## An internship she never applied for

By her later semesters, she had gone deep enough into a seminar topic that she could hold her own in a room of specialists. At an AI event, she sat next to someone she didn't recognize and spent the session asking sharp, prepared questions, the same habit of researching a speaker beforehand and coming with real questions that she'd first learned before attending a different conference. The stranger turned out to run a company. He messaged her on LinkedIn two days later. A three-round interview followed. She never sent a resume or filled out an application.

## What she does now

She's no longer doing purely technical AI work. After trying it professionally, she realized it wasn't where her strengths were, and moved into a role that's closer to business analysis and community management, work built on the same skills she'd been practicing since her first TinkerHub event: talking to people, holding a room, solving a problem in front of you instead of waiting for the textbook version of it.

Her advice to a first-year version of herself isn't a five-step plan. It's smaller than that: figure out what you actually like by trying things in public, and put yourself around people who are building something, because the room you sit in changes what you end up doing.
